The question

Livable city, uneven change.

Copenhagen is often described as one of Europe’s most livable cities. But city-wide averages hide a quieter spatial story: the international population has grown strongly since 1995, while different neighbourhoods have followed different paths.

What counts as “international” here?

Residents with western or non-western immigrant/descendant background are grouped together for the main share metric. The goal is descriptive: to show how neighbourhood composition changed over time without relying on politically loaded labels.

How to read the story

The first sections guide you through the main findings. Later sections open up interactive charts and maps, so you can inspect the data yourself by district, year, and origin country.

1 · The city-level trend

The share of international residents more than doubled.

Across Copenhagen’s ten main districts, the international share rose from 13.0% in 1995 to 28.2% in 2025. The shift is gradual, not a single shock, although the years around 2015 stand out in the curve.

The city as a whole became steadily more international over three decades.
2 · The district split

Same city, different trajectories.

The overall trend hides large neighbourhood differences. By 2025, Brønshøj-Husum had the highest international share at 38.4%, while Vanløse had the lowest at 20.2%.

3 · Pattern finding

Three neighbourhood profiles emerge.

Using KMeans clustering on non-western share snapshots, the districts fall into three interpretable groups: low, medium, and high internationalisation. The algorithm is technical behind the scenes, but the result is intuitive on the map of Copenhagen.

Method and limits

What the website shows and what it cannot claim.

Data

District-level annual population counts from 1995 to 2025 across Copenhagen’s ten main boroughs, grouped by ancestry category. Country-of-origin data is analysed only at city level.

Analysis

The story uses shares, trend lines, KMeans clustering, PCA visualisation, and simple linear forecasting. The technical details stay in the notebook; this page focuses on readable public communication.

Important limitation

The income variable appears city-level rather than truly district-level in the notebook, so district income maps/scatterplots are not used as evidence in this website.

Better next step

A true borough polygon choropleth and finer sub-district data would make the geography more accurate than point-based heat maps.
